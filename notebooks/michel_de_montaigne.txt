Michel de Montaigne (1533–1592): Overview

Biography
Michel Eyquem de Montaigne was born on February 28, 1533, in the château of Montaigne, near Bordeaux, in the Kingdom of France. A member of the minor nobility, he received a humanist education: Latin was his principal language until about age six, and he later studied law. Montaigne served as a magistrate and as mayor of Bordeaux before retiring to his library to devote himself to writing. He traveled through Europe in the 1580s. Montaigne died on September 13, 1592.

Major Work
- Essays (Essais): First published in 1580 and expanded in later editions (1582, 1588, 1595 posthumous). The Essays are a collection of short, personal reflections on a broad range of topics — from the mundane to the philosophical — written in a conversational and intimate style. The work did not follow a strict system; instead, Montaigne explored ideas by examining himself and the human condition.

Philosophical Themes
- Skepticism: Montaigne is often associated with philosophical skepticism. Influenced by the rediscovery of Sextus Empiricus and the revival of Pyrrhonism during the Renaissance, Montaigne questioned the certainty of human knowledge and urged intellectual humility. His famous motto, "Que sais-je?" ("What do I know?"), captures this stance.

- Self-Examination and the Personal: Montaigne pioneered a form of writing that used the self as the starting point for philosophical inquiry. He examined his own thoughts, habits, and experiences as a way to probe universal human truths. This introspective method marked a major shift in essayistic and philosophical literature.

- Humanism and Moderation: As a Renaissance humanist, Montaigne emphasized the dignity, complexity, and fallibility of human beings. He favored moderation and practical wisdom (prudence) over dogmatism and extreme positions.

- Relativism and Cultural Critique: In essays such as "Of Cannibals," Montaigne challenged ethnocentric assumptions and argued that customs are relative. He used comparative description to question claims of superiority between cultures.

- Education and Upbringing: In "Of the Education of Children," Montaigne criticized rote learning and advocated for an education focused on developing judgment, moral character, and speaking ability.

- Friendship: Montaigne wrote movingly about friendship, especially his deep bond with Étienne de La Boétie, which he considered an ideal and almost sacred form of companionship.

- Mortality and Death: Montaigne confronted death candidly, arguing that constant reflection on mortality can free us from fear and help us live better.

Style and Method
Montaigne's prose is notable for its conversational tone, digressions, personal anecdotes, quotations (in Latin and Greek as well as other sources), and rhetorical questioning. He valued honesty and self-revelation; his Essays feel like a record of an ongoing internal dialogue rather than a systematic philosophical treatise.

Influence and Legacy
- The modern essay: Montaigne is credited with developing the essay as a literary form — a flexible, personal mode of writing that blends philosophy, anecdote, and reflection.

- Early modern and modern thought: His skepticism and emphasis on subjectivity influenced thinkers such as Descartes (indirectly), Pascal, Bacon, and later Enlightenment and existential figures. Writers and philosophers across Europe, from Shakespeare to Nietzsche, acknowledged Montaigne's influence.

- Literary and psychological insight: Montaigne's attention to individual psychology and the complexities of character anticipated later developments in literature and philosophy.

Notable Quotations (in translation)
- "What do I know?" (Que sais-je?)
- "I am myself the matter of my book."
- "A man who fears suffering is already suffering from what he fears." 
- "My life has been full of terrible misfortunes most of which never happened."

Further Reading
- Montaigne, The Complete Essays, translated by M.A. Screech or Donald M. Frame.
- Pierre Villey, Montaigne: A Critical Biography.
- Sarah Bakewell, How to Live: A Life of Montaigne in One Question and Twenty Attempts at an Answer (popular biography emphasizing Montaigne's ideas).

Short Summary
Michel de Montaigne was a French Renaissance humanist and the creator of the modern essay. Through intimate self-examination and skeptical inquiry, he explored human nature, knowledge, education, friendship, and death. His candid, digressive style and philosophical humility left a lasting imprint on literature and thought.
